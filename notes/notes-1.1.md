# 1.1 - On Craft and Social Justice

September 2, 2025: The weather drapes a sleek, thermobaric flank on one side of today and a chilled muzzle on the other, a metaphor meant to make you feel something critical about the mundane activity of gathering for class in a university setting. Barely a phrase, but it has an internal logic, a politics, a poetics, which I list separately for clarity's sake but which are as entangled as our veins and nerves. Or nerves and veins. Which logically says the same thing but affectively does not. What you learned in elementary school mathematics is true here. The order of operations matters. So do the variables. So does the ability to reverse-engineer a proof. Both writing and activism are about strategically breaking rules. Whether stylistic choice or ideology, you can't do this without first knowing what those rules are. 

Do you feel a little uncertain, a little yes-my-curiosity-has-been-piqued, a little excited, a little filled with dread, confused? Good.

I want to welcome you to WS 297V: Feminist Disability Justice. Call me Dr. Mani. I'm an Eelam Tamil American. I've had chronic pain and fatigue for 20 years, taught for 19, written for 30, and have published work in creative and scholarly forums that in part enacts resistance by resisting classification itself. Online, you can call me my server name, Legendary Boon, a reference to the *Hades* video game series.

This course focuses on genres of resistance &mdash; particularly the lyric essay &mdash; and what it means to write for social justice. Part-webinar, part-roleplay, part-workshop, you'll be introduced to the elements of poetry, fiction, and creative nonfiction through reading, writing, and individual and cooperative play. Since social justice writing is writing that does something to its audience, you'll regularly engage with, interpret (and create!) texts that model how content and form act together on readers. In doing so, you'll consider questions like: How have we been shaped, personally and politically, by reading and writing? How have systems of power and oppression shaped who and how we read; who and what we write about; how we compose and perceive our compositions; and which genres we consider "legitimate" genres? What potential impact can we &mdash; through what we write &mdash; have on our communities and institutions?

And of course, I can't introduce this class without acknowledging that the U.S. is in the grips of social upheaval that is contingent on a lack of knowledge, a lack of curiosity, an obstinacy towards learning, an unwillingness to listen to the elders who formed the political bodies of similar social movements before, media illiteracy, and the unchecked removal of women's, gender, and sexuality studies and antiracist curriculum from the education system. We're in the process of losing a free and open Internet under the guise of age verification legislation for pornography sites, which has already been used to ban minors from global politics and sex education forums and queer communities, because an uninformed populace is one that's easy to control.

The assigned readings and writing exercises aren't meant to make you feel like a better person, which is what "inspiring awareness and empathy" can mean around social justice. Resistance is grueling, often thankless feeling work that requires coalition building and the suppression of ego. Like Mariame Kaba says, hope is a discipline. We'll read, write, and play through building coalitions and kinds of hope and resistance in seemingly hopeless scenarios.

Now to circle back to our metaphoric opening: "The weather drapes a sleek, thermobaric flank on one side of today and a chilled muzzle on the other," and I'd like you to post, free-form and stream-of-consciousness and also in response to my rhetorical questions, in #classroom. 

Do you feel something from this metaphor? Where do you feel the identification of a feeling first, even as an inarticulate beginning? Where do you feel that identification become coherent? How do you even begin to figure something like that out?

This class is about learning how to figure something like that out, learning about the role that rhetoric &mdash; whose canons for effective communication include invention (coming up with something in prewriting), arrangement (organizing it in prewriting), style (making craft decisions during drafting), memory (recalling information, either for oral presentation or for revision purposes), and delivery (how the message is presented) &mdash; plays in creative writing for social justice.

Metaphors ask us to reflect on the relationships between things. So do the tiny white spaces between sentences, and the larger spaces between paragraphs. Form, also, asks us to reflect on relationships &mdash; specifically between design and "argument."

Understand also that an argument, contrary to what you might have been taught, doesn't just mean thesis or aggression or debate or persuasion but also the less definable ways that texts affect readers. Texts always argue. Effective texts understand that the form contributes to or performs the argument.
